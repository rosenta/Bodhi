# Brand Voice Guide

> The voice of Project Bodhi is the friend who reads philosophy at midnight and explains it to you over coffee the next morning. Not a guru. Not a professor. A fellow traveler who happens to have a flashlight.

---

## Core Voice Attributes

### 1. Conversational, Not Preachy
You are talking WITH someone, not AT them. You are in a conversation, not delivering a sermon. The reader/viewer should feel like they are in a late-night conversation with a curious, well-read friend who is genuinely excited about what they have found.

### 2. Wonder-Filled, Not Worshipful
There is genuine awe at the depth and precision of these texts. But it is the awe of discovery, not devotion. We are not prostrating before Shankara -- we are marveling at the fact that someone figured this out 1,200 years ago and it still hits harder than any TED Talk.

### 3. Grounded, Not Floating
Every concept connects to real life: your job, your 3 AM anxiety, your relationship patterns, your phone addiction. We never leave ideas in the abstract. If a verse does not connect to something you have actually experienced, we have failed.

### 4. Rigorous, Not Academic
We respect the source material. Translations are accurate. Sanskrit terms are used correctly. But we never hide behind jargon. If a concept cannot be explained in plain language, we have not understood it well enough.

### 5. Inclusive, Not Gatekeeping
This wisdom belongs to everyone. It is not "for Hindus," it is not "for Indians," it is not "for people who already know Sanskrit." It is for anyone who has ever asked "Who am I?" -- and that includes everyone.

## 10 Sample Captions in the Right Voice

### 1. (For Verse 2 -- rarity of human birth)
"You beat odds of 1 in 400 trillion to be born. Not born rich. Not born famous. Just... born. Human. With the capacity to sit here and wonder: what am I? Shankara wrote this 1,200 years ago, and it hits different when you actually let it land. You have this impossibly rare chance to figure out what you are. Most people will spend it worrying about their Instagram reach. What are you going to do with yours?"

### 2. (For Verse 20 -- Brahman is real, world is appearance)
"Here is the most concise summary of 1,200 years of Vedanta philosophy: the awareness witnessing your life never changes. Everything else does. Your body. Your job. Your relationships. Your beliefs. Even your personality. But that quiet presence watching it all? Same in childhood. Same now. Same in your last breath. Shankara says: learn to tell the difference between the screen and the movie. The screen is you."

### 3. (For Verse 78 -- five animals, five senses)
"A deer dies chasing a beautiful sound. An elephant dies seeking touch. A moth dies fixated on light. A fish dies biting the bait. A bee dies trapped in a flower's scent. Each falls to just one sense. You? You carry all five. Plus a phone engineered by the smartest people on earth to hijack every single one of them. Shankara did not have Instagram. But he saw you coming."

### 4. (For Verse 174 -- mind creates bondage and freedom)
"Your mind is not your enemy. It is your only tool. The same mind that replays embarrassing moments at 2 AM is the same mind that can ask: 'Who is the one remembering?' The wind that gathered the storm clouds is the same wind that clears the sky. You do not need a transplant. You need a redirect."

### 5. (For Verse 199 -- rope-snake)
"You have been afraid of a snake that was never there. The thing about the rope-snake teaching is not that it makes you feel silly for being afraid. It is that the moment you see the rope -- truly see it -- the fear is not 'overcome' or 'managed.' It is simply gone. It was never real. How much of what you are afraid of right now falls into the same category?"

### 6. (For Yoga Sutra 1.2 -- yoga defined)
"I am going to ruin yoga for you. Or maybe fix it. Sutra 1.2: 'Yoga is the cessation of the fluctuations of the mind.' Not 'yoga is a really good hamstring stretch.' Not 'yoga is an aesthetic for your Instagram grid.' Yoga is learning to be still inside. The postures are page 3 of a 196-page manual. We have been reading page 3 on repeat for 30 years."

### 7. (For Verse 325 -- duckweed)
"You know that feeling when you meditate, feel genuinely peaceful, pick up your phone, and 90 seconds later you have forgotten you were ever calm? That is not failure. That is duckweed. The Vivekachudamani says Maya covers your clarity the instant you stop paying attention. Not because you are weak. Because that is literally what Maya does. The practice is not 'achieve clarity once.' It is 'clear the duckweed. Again. And again.'"

### 8. (For Verse 53 -- only you can free yourself)
"Your parents can wire you money. Your therapist can give you a framework. Your best friend can listen at midnight. But the one chain that matters -- the chain of not knowing who you are -- nobody else can break that. Not because no one cares. Because it is YOUR lock, and the key is inside YOU. That sounds lonely. But Shankara says it is actually the most empowering thing you will ever hear."

### 9. (For Verse 136 -- pot and sky)
"When a clay pot breaks, the space inside it does not shatter. It just... merges back into the sky it was always part of. Your body is the pot. The awareness inside it is the sky. The pot will break. The sky never will. This verse is not about denying death. It is about questioning who is actually doing the dying."

### 10. (For the Kleshas -- Yoga Sutra 2.3)
"Patanjali said there are five things causing all your suffering. Five. Not five hundred. And the root of all five is the same: not knowing who you are. That is Avidya. From Avidya grows ego, clinging, avoidance, and the fear of death. Everything else -- every argument, every anxiety, every 3 AM spiral -- is branches on this tree. The good news? You do not need to trim every branch. You need to see the root."

## 10 Examples of What NOT to Sound Like

### 1. The Guru Voice (preachy, authoritarian)
"The Supreme Lord Shankara has blessed us with the divine knowledge of the Vivekachudamani. We must surrender ourselves completely to the lotus feet of the Guru and follow His sacred teachings with utmost devotion and humility."

**Why this fails**: This is worship, not wisdom. It puts Shankara on a pedestal that alienates the exact audience we want to reach. The "spiritual but not religious" seeker will bounce in 2 seconds.

### 2. The Academic Voice (dry, distanced)
"Verse 20 of the Vivekachudamani articulates the fundamental ontological position of Advaita Vedanta, wherein Brahman is posited as the sole ultimately real entity (paramarthika sat), while the phenomenal world (vyavaharika) is relegated to the status of mithya, or false appearance."

**Why this fails**: Technically accurate. Emotionally dead. Nobody shares this. Nobody feels this. The person going through an existential crisis at midnight will not be helped by the word "ontological."

### 3. The New Age Voice (vague, ungrounded)
"Open your heart chakra and let the vibrations of the universe flow through you. You are a being of pure light and love. The Vivekachudamani is just another way of saying: raise your vibration, babe."

**Why this fails**: This strips the text of its intellectual rigor and precision. Shankara was not talking about "vibes." He was making precise philosophical arguments. This voice attracts followers who want comfort, not transformation.

### 4. The Motivational Speaker Voice (hype without depth)
"LISTEN UP. You are LIMITLESS. You are UNSTOPPABLE. Shankara KNEW this. He PROVED it. You just need to BELIEVE in yourself and MANIFEST your highest self. Are you READY to level up?!?!"

**Why this fails**: All energy, no insight. The Vivekachudamani is not a pep talk. It is a careful, systematic dismantling of everything you think you are. Hype without substance is the opposite of Viveka.

### 5. The Gatekeeping Voice (elitist, exclusionary)
"To truly understand the Vivekachudamani, one must first study the Prasthanatraya, have a solid grounding in Mimamsa, and ideally be able to read the original Sanskrit. Otherwise, you are only getting a surface understanding."

**Why this fails**: This is technically true in a traditional gurukula setting. It is also the fastest way to make sure nobody new ever engages with this text. We are building a gateway, not a gate.

### 6. The Clickbait Voice (all hook, no substance)
"This ONE VERSE will make you RICH, HAPPY, and ENLIGHTENED (verse 20 SHOCKED me) (not clickbait)"

**Why this fails**: Cheapens the material. Spiritual seekers can smell inauthenticity instantly. It might get a click, but it will get an unfollow 30 seconds later.

### 7. The Performative Woke Voice (signaling without substance)
"As we decolonize our understanding of yoga and center indigenous Hindu voices in the discourse around Vedantic epistemology, it is important to acknowledge the problematic power dynamics inherent in..."

**Why this fails**: Important conversations, wrong venue. This project is about making the texts accessible and transformative. Centering academic discourse over direct experience is the opposite of what Shankara taught.

### 8. The "Spiritual Influencer" Voice (aesthetic over substance)
"morning rituals. sage. crystals. vivekachudamani on the nightstand. oat milk latte. inner peace is an aesthetic."

**Why this fails**: Reduces a text about the nature of consciousness to a lifestyle brand. The Vivekachudamani is not decor.

### 9. The Defensive Religious Voice (apologetic, reactive)
"For those who say Hinduism is 'just mythology,' the Vivekachudamani PROVES that our tradition has the most advanced philosophy in the world. Western philosophy is shallow compared to what Shankara achieved."

**Why this fails**: Defensiveness signals insecurity. The text does not need defending. It needs presenting. Comparative claims ("better than Western philosophy") alienate the cross-tradition audience that is our biggest growth vector.

### 10. The Overly Casual Voice (irreverent to the point of disrespect)
"lol Shankara was basically the original sigma male grinding on that vedanta hustle. bro literally told his student 'skill issue' when the dude was having a mental breakdown"

**Why this fails**: There is a line between making ancient wisdom accessible and reducing it to memes. Shankara can handle humor. But the student coming to the guru in despair (verses 35-40) is a moment of genuine human vulnerability. Reducing that to "skill issue" disrespects both the text and the person watching who feels the same way.

## How to Talk About Sanskrit Terms

### The Golden Rule: Translate Immediately, Every Time

Never use a Sanskrit term without its English meaning in the same sentence. Not in the next sentence. In the SAME sentence.

**Right**: "This is called Maya -- the power of cosmic illusion that makes you see what is not there."
**Right**: "Shankara calls this Adhyasa, or superimposition -- gluing your identity onto things that are not you."
**Right**: "Vairagya (dispassion) is not about giving things up. It is about not being owned by them."

**Wrong**: "This is what Shankara calls Adhyasa." [reader Googles it, gets confused, leaves]
**Wrong**: "To achieve Moksha, one must cultivate Viveka and Vairagya through Sadhana." [this is a sentence for people who already know everything]

### The Format Template

Use this pattern consistently: **[Sanskrit term] -- [English meaning in plain language]**

Or, if the term is well-known enough to lead with: **[English concept] -- what the text calls [Sanskrit term]**

Examples:
- "discrimination between the real and the unreal -- what Shankara calls Viveka"
- "Maya -- the invisible operating system that generates everything you think is real"
- "the five Kleshas (root afflictions) that Patanjali says cause ALL your suffering"

### When to Use the Sanskrit and When to Skip It

**Always use the Sanskrit** when:
- Introducing a key concept for the first time (it becomes part of the viewer's vocabulary)
- The Sanskrit word is shorter or more precise than its English equivalent (Atman, Maya, Karma)
- The word appears in the original verse being discussed

**Skip the Sanskrit** when:
- The English is clearer and you are making a point, not teaching vocabulary
- You have already introduced the term earlier in the same post/reel
- Using it would require a parenthetical that breaks the emotional flow

### Pronunciation Notes in Reels

When saying a Sanskrit word for the first time in a reel, briefly slow down and enunciate. Do not do a full phonetic breakdown (save that for YouTube deep-dives), but make it clear enough that viewers feel confident repeating it.

## How to Handle Comments

### The Seeker
Someone genuinely curious, asking questions, sharing their experience.

**Respond with**:
- Warmth and encouragement
- A follow-up question to deepen their inquiry
- A specific verse or practice if relevant

**Example comment**: "This hit me hard. I have been feeling exactly like that student. What do I do next?"

**Response**: "The fact that you feel this? Shankara says that IS the beginning. The next verse (45) literally says: 'Fear not. There is a way.' If you want to go deeper, try sitting for 5 minutes today and asking: 'Who is the one feeling lost?' Not to answer it -- just to sit with it. Let me know what comes up."

### The Troll
Dismissive, mocking, or provocative without substance.

**Respond with**:
- Grace and zero defensiveness
- A single substantive point, then move on
- Never engage more than once

**Example comment**: "lol ancient fairy tales for people who cant deal with reality"

**Response**: "Fair pushback. Though Shankara would probably ask: what do you mean by 'reality'? That is literally the question this text spends 580 verses investigating. If you are curious, Verse 20 is a good entry point."

Do NOT: delete the comment, argue, be sarcastic, or ignore it entirely. One calm, substantive response serves the silent audience watching the exchange.

### The Devotee
Someone deeply embedded in the Hindu tradition, potentially protective of the text.

**Respond with**:
- Respect for their depth of knowledge
- Humility about your own learning
- An invitation to contribute, not a correction

**Example comment**: "This translation misses the deeper meaning. Shankara is referencing the Mandukya Upanishad here and the three states of consciousness. You should study more before teaching."

**Response**: "You are absolutely right that this connects to the Mandukya -- thank you for pointing that out. I kept this short for the format, but the three-states connection is something I want to do a full deep-dive on. Would love your perspective when I do. What do you think is the most important thing people miss about this verse?"

Do NOT: get defensive, dismiss their knowledge, or claim authority you do not have. Turn critics into collaborators.

### The Skeptic
Intellectually engaged but questioning the validity or relevance.

**Respond with**:
- Respect for their critical thinking (this IS Viveka in action)
- Specific, grounded responses (not hand-waving)
- An honest "I do not know" when you do not know

**Example comment**: "This is interesting but how do you verify any of this? It is all subjective experience. You cannot prove the Self exists."

**Response**: "Honestly, great question -- and Shankara would respect it. He actually addresses this: the Self is not an object you can prove like a chair. It is the subject doing the proving. You cannot step outside awareness to look at awareness. His argument is: try to deny that you exist, and who is doing the denying? That said, he also says do not take his word for it. The whole point is direct experience, not belief."

### The "Which Text Should I Read?" Person

**Response template**: "If you want to start, I would suggest reading 10 key verses with modern interpretations rather than all 580 at once. I have a free PDF with 10 of the most powerful verses -- link in my bio. If you want the full text, the Gita Press Gorakhpur edition is excellent and affordable. For English, the Central Chinmaya Mission Trust edition is very readable. But honestly? One verse deeply understood beats 580 skimmed. Start with Verse 20."

## Cultural Sensitivity Guidelines

### 1. Respect the Tradition Without Being Captured by It

The Vivekachudamani and Yoga Sutras come from Hindu philosophical traditions. This is a fact, not an opinion, and it deserves respect. We acknowledge this lineage clearly and consistently.

But we also recognize that these texts speak to universal human questions -- Who am I? Why do I suffer? What is real? -- that transcend any single religious identity. Shankara's arguments do not require you to be Hindu to examine them. Patanjali's analysis of the mind does not require a temple membership.

**Practically**: Always credit the tradition. Never strip the context. But also never suggest someone needs to convert, chant, or perform rituals to engage with these ideas.

### 2. We Are Not a Hindu Outreach Project

This is not religious content. It is philosophical content rooted in Hindu traditions. The distinction matters. We are not trying to make anyone Hindu. We are presenting ideas that happen to originate from Hindu thought and letting people engage with them on their own terms.

**Practically**: Avoid language like "our scriptures," "our gods," "our tradition." Use "this text says," "Shankara argues," "Patanjali's analysis suggests." Frame as investigation, not instruction.

### 3. Translation Integrity

Every translation should be:
- Faithful to the Gita Press edition as the base
- Cross-referenced with at least one other reputable translation
- Clearly marked when a "modern interpretation" diverges from literal meaning

**Practically**: In every post that includes a translation, use this structure:
- Verse (Sanskrit)
- Translation (as faithful as possible)
- Modern Interpretation (clearly labeled, acknowledging that this is the creator's reading, not the definitive one)

### 4. Do Not Exoticize

Sanskrit is beautiful. Indian philosophy is profound. But do not treat it as exotic, mystical, or "other." Present it with the same clarity and directness you would present Stoicism or existentialism. The moment you add mystical fog, you lose the intellectual audience and you patronize the tradition.

**Practically**: No mystical stock images (third eyes, galaxies, glowing chakras). No Indian-accented voiceover for "authenticity." No sitars playing in the background of every reel. Use the aesthetic from the brand guidelines (dark, clean, modern, minimal) and let the ideas provide the depth.

### 5. Handle the "Cultural Appropriation" Question Honestly

If someone accuses the project of cultural appropriation:

**Response template**: "I take this concern seriously. These texts come from Hindu philosophical traditions, and I acknowledge and respect that lineage in every post. My goal is not to extract ideas from their context but to present them with accuracy, credit, and care to an audience that might never encounter them otherwise. Shankara wrote these verses to be studied and taught. The Vivekachudamani is a teaching text -- it was literally designed to be transmitted. If anything I present is inaccurate or disrespectful, I genuinely want to know. Tell me and I will fix it."

### 6. Do Not Flatten Differences

When drawing parallels between Vedanta and other traditions (Stoicism, Buddhism, psychology), be precise. Do not say "it is the same thing." Say "there is an interesting parallel here, and also an important difference."

**Example**: "Patanjali's Kleshas and CBT's cognitive distortions are addressing similar territory -- patterns of thinking that cause suffering. But there is a key difference: CBT aims to replace distorted thoughts with more accurate ones. Patanjali aims to recognize that you are not your thoughts at all. Similar diagnosis. Different prescription."

### 7. Gender-Inclusive Language

The source texts use masculine language and address a male student. Our modern interpretation is for everyone.

**Practically**: Use "they/you/one" instead of "he/him" in interpretations. When referring to the disciple in the narrative, use "the student" or "the seeker," not "he." When quoting translations directly, keep the original pronouns but note the context when relevant.

## Voice Checklist (Use Before Every Post)

Before publishing any content, run it through these 7 questions:

1. **Would I say this to a friend over coffee?** If not, rewrite it until you would.
2. **Does every Sanskrit term have an immediate English translation?** If not, add it.
3. **Does this connect to something real in someone's life?** If it stays abstract, ground it.
4. **Am I explaining or preaching?** If preaching, step back into curiosity.
5. **Would a 25-year-old agnostic find this interesting?** If not, you have lost the plot.
6. **Would a traditional Vedanta scholar find this disrespectful?** If yes, add nuance.
7. **Does this invite further inquiry or shut it down?** Always end with an open door.
