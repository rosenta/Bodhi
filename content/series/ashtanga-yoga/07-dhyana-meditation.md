# Limb 7: Dhyana -- Meditation

The word "meditation" has been stretched to meaninglessness. It now covers guided visualizations, body scans, apps with ocean sounds, corporate mindfulness seminars, and sitting in a dimly lit room thinking about not thinking. Patanjali would not recognize any of this as dhyana.

What he means by meditation is something rare, specific, and transformative -- and it is not something you do. It is something that happens when dharana succeeds.

## What Patanjali Actually Says

Sutra 3.2: "Tatra pratyaya-ekatanata dhyanam." In that place (the object of concentration), when the flow of cognition becomes continuous and unbroken, that is dhyana.

Read it carefully. Dhyana is not a technique. It is a state -- the state that arises when concentration stops being interrupted. If dharana is placing the mind on the object and repeatedly bringing it back, dhyana is the moment the leash disappears and the mind stays of its own accord. The effort drops away. The stream of awareness flows toward the object the way water flows downhill: naturally, continuously, without force.

This is a qualitative shift, not a quantitative one. It is not "better concentration." It is the dissolution of the gap between you and what you are attending to. In dharana, there is a meditator, an effort, and an object. In dhyana, the effort vanishes. The meditator and the object begin to merge. Not intellectually. Experientially. The boundary between "I" and "what I am aware of" thins.

## The Common Misconception

The biggest misunderstanding is that meditation is something you can schedule. "I meditated for twenty minutes this morning." You may have practiced dharana for twenty minutes. You may have sat with eyes closed for twenty minutes. But dhyana is not on a timer. It arrives on its own terms, often fleetingly, after sustained concentration has prepared the ground.

Another misconception: that dhyana should look or feel a certain way. People expect fireworks -- visions, lights, ecstatic sensations. Genuine dhyana is often unremarkable from the outside and indescribable from the inside. It is characterized by absorption, not excitement. Time distortion is common -- twenty minutes feel like two, or two feel like twenty. There is a quality of seamlessness, of the mind flowing without interruption, like oil poured from one vessel to another. No drops. No splashes. Just a continuous stream.

The most harmful misconception is that dhyana requires you to stop thinking. It does not. It requires that the flow of awareness toward the object is unbroken. Thoughts may arise at the periphery, but they do not capture attention. They are like sounds heard from deep underwater -- present, but unable to pull you to the surface. You do not fight thoughts. You become so absorbed in the object that thoughts lose their grip naturally.

## The Real Practice

You cannot practice dhyana the way you practice asana or pranayama. You can only create the conditions for it and wait. Those conditions are the six limbs that precede it.

What you can do is recognize the moments when dhyana naturally occurs in your life. Have you ever been so absorbed in music that you forgot where you were? So lost in conversation that an hour vanished? So engaged in a task that the sense of "I" temporarily dissolved? Those were tastes of dhyana -- unstructured, accidental, but real.

The yogic project is to cultivate this capacity deliberately and direct it inward. Not toward a song or a conversation, but toward the breath, a mantra, or the space of awareness itself. When you sit for meditation and there is a stretch -- even fifteen seconds -- where effort disappears and you and the object are simply flowing together, that is dhyana. It does not need to last an hour. It needs to be recognized.

Recognition matters because without it, people sit in dharana for decades and never notice when dhyana arrives. They are too busy trying. Dhyana requires the paradox of effort that knows when to release itself.

## Try This Today

During your next period of concentration (formal or informal -- even deep reading counts), watch for the moment when effort drops. It will be brief. You were trying to focus, and suddenly you are focused without trying. The gap between you and the object narrows. There is a feeling of flow, ease, and absorption. When you notice it -- and the noticing may end it -- do not be disappointed. Simply note: "That was dhyana." Then return to dharana and let it come again. The noticing trains the mind to recognize its own deepest capacity.
