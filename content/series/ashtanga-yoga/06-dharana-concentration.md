# Limb 6: Dharana -- Concentration

You have been told to "focus" ten thousand times in your life. Teachers, parents, bosses, motivational speakers -- everyone agrees that concentration is valuable. Almost no one teaches you how to do it. Patanjali does.

But what he means by concentration is not what your productivity guru means. Dharana is not about getting more done. It is about binding the mind so completely to one point that the mind itself begins to dissolve.

## What Patanjali Actually Says

Sutra 3.1 is deceptively simple: "Dharana is the binding of the mind to a single place." Desha-bandha. Fixing the mind's attention on one spot -- the breath at the nostrils, the space between the eyebrows, the heart center, a mantra, a flame, an image. One point. Nothing else.

The prerequisite is critical. Sutra 2.53 states that the mind becomes fit for dharana only after pranayama has done its work. You cannot concentrate a turbulent mind any more than you can see your reflection in churning water. The first five limbs calm the water. Dharana is the act of looking.

Notice what dharana is not. It is not thinking about something. It is not analyzing an object. It is not contemplating a concept. It is the sustained, non-discursive fixing of attention on a single point. When you practice dharana and a thought arises about the object, you have left dharana. When you return to the bare, direct contact with the object, you have come back.

## The Common Misconception

The modern world confuses concentration with intensity. "I really concentrated on that report for four hours." What most people call concentration is actually rapid switching between related tasks with high arousal. True dharana is something most people have never experienced for more than a few seconds.

Try it now. Fix your attention on the sensation of your next breath entering your nostrils. Just the sensation. Not the thought about the sensation. Not the label "breathing." The raw, direct feeling of air touching skin. How long before your mind wanders? Three seconds? Five? Ten?

That gap between intention and execution is the territory of dharana. It is humbling. People who can run marathons and build companies discover that holding their attention on a single point for sixty unbroken seconds is nearly impossible. This is not a failure of character. It is an untrained capacity, like trying to run a marathon without ever having jogged.

The other misconception is that dharana should feel like effort. Initially, yes. But Patanjali's system is designed so that by the time you reach dharana, the outer limbs have prepared you. Ethical clarity (yama/niyama) removes guilt and inner conflict. Physical stability (asana) removes bodily distraction. Breath mastery (pranayama) calms the nervous system. Sense withdrawal (pratyahara) removes external pull. With all of this in place, dharana becomes the natural next step, not a forced march.

## The Real Practice

Dharana is the simplest practice in the world and the hardest to maintain. Choose an object. A candle flame, the breath, a mantra. Set a timer for five minutes. Hold your attention on that object alone.

When the mind wanders -- and it will, dozens of times -- bring it back. Without judgment. Without frustration. Without commentary. The act of returning is not a failure. It is the practice. Every return strengthens the muscle of attention. The wandering is the weight. The return is the rep.

Over weeks and months, the intervals between wandering lengthen. Where there were three seconds of unbroken attention, there are ten. Then thirty. Then minutes. The mind begins to settle into the object like a bird settling onto a branch. Effortlessly. Naturally. This is when dharana starts to deepen into something else entirely.

## Try This Today

Set a timer for five minutes. Choose one object: the feeling of air entering your nostrils. Close your eyes. Each time you notice your mind has wandered, make a quiet internal note -- "wandered" -- and return to the sensation. Count the wanderings if you want. Most people count forty or more in five minutes. That number is not the problem. It is the diagnosis. And awareness of the diagnosis is the beginning of the cure.
