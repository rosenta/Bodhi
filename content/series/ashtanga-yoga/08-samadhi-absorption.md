# Limb 8: Samadhi -- Absorption

Everything has been building to this. The ethics cleared the moral debris. The observances tuned the inner instrument. The steady seat freed the body. The breath calmed the nervous system. The senses turned inward. Concentration fixed the mind. Meditation dissolved the effort. And now the final threshold: the point where the one who meditates and the object of meditation are no longer two.

This is samadhi. And it is both simpler and stranger than anything you expect.

## What Patanjali Actually Says

Sutra 3.3: "When that same meditation shines forth as the object alone, as if devoid of its own nature, it is samadhi."

Unpack that slowly. In dhyana, there is still a subtle sense of "I am meditating on this." In samadhi, even that dissolves. The mind becomes so transparent, so perfectly shaped to the object, that it is as if the mind itself disappears. Only the essence of the object remains -- luminous, direct, without the filter of the meditator's personality, history, or conceptual framework.

Patanjali calls dharana, dhyana, and samadhi together "samyama" (sutra 3.4) -- a single integrated movement from focused attention to absorption. They are not three separate practices but three phases of one deepening process, the way ice, water, and steam are three phases of one substance.

But samadhi is not the end. Patanjali describes multiple levels. Samprajnata samadhi (with cognitive content) and asamprajnata samadhi (without). Sabija (with seed -- with a subtle object) and nirbija (seedless -- beyond all objects). The deepest samadhi is what sutra 1.2 points toward: the complete cessation of the modifications of the mind. And sutra 1.3: "Then the Seer abides in its own true nature."

This is the destination of the entire system. Not a blissful experience. Not a cosmic vision. The Seer -- pure awareness itself -- resting as what it has always been, without the distortion of any mental modification.

## The Common Misconception

Samadhi has been mythologized into inaccessibility. It is treated as something for saints in Himalayan caves, not for ordinary people with jobs and families. This reverence, while understandable, becomes an obstacle. If you believe samadhi is impossible for you, you will never create the conditions for it.

The opposite misconception is equally damaging: reducing samadhi to any pleasant meditative state. "I had such a deep meditation, I think I was in samadhi." Maybe. But samadhi is not characterized by pleasant feelings. It is characterized by the temporary dissolution of the subject-object divide. You can feel wonderful and still be firmly in duality. Samadhi is what happens when the duality itself drops.

Another subtle error: treating samadhi as a permanent achievement. Most practitioners experience samadhi in brief, temporary flashes before returning to ordinary consciousness. This is normal. The practice is to make these flashes more frequent and more stable until, eventually, the recognition of one's true nature becomes unshakeable -- what the tradition calls sahaja samadhi, the natural state.

## The Real Practice

You cannot manufacture samadhi. You can only remove the obstacles to it. This is the entire logic of the eight limbs: each one removes a specific class of obstacle. Ethical disorder disturbs the mind -- the yamas handle it. Inner sloth and impurity cloud the mind -- the niyamas handle it. Physical discomfort distracts -- asana handles it. Respiratory turbulence agitates -- pranayama handles it. Sensory pull fragments -- pratyahara handles it. Mental restlessness scatters -- dharana handles it. The sense of being a separate meditator interferes -- dhyana thins it.

What remains when all obstacles are removed? Not a new experience. The original condition. Awareness, aware of itself, without a story attached.

This is why the Yoga Sutras begin with the definition (1.2) rather than ending with it. The destination is already present. The eight limbs do not create something new. They uncover what was never absent.

## Try This Today

This may sound anticlimactic after discussing the pinnacle of yogic achievement, but the most useful thing you can do is this: for sixty seconds, stop trying to be anything. Do not meditate. Do not concentrate. Do not breathe deliberately. Do not even try to be aware. Simply stop. Let everything be as it is. The body sits. The breath moves. Thoughts drift. And something -- something that has never not been here -- notices all of it without any effort at all. That effortless noticing is closer to samadhi than any dramatic experience. It is what you are when you forget to pretend you are something else.
