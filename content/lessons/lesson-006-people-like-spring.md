# The People Who Change the Season of a Room

> शान्ता महान्तो निवसन्ति सन्तो
> वसन्तवल्लोकहितं चरन्तः।
> तीर्णाः स्वयं भीमभवार्णवं जना-
> नहेतुनान्यानपि तारयन्तः॥ ३९॥
>
> *Great, peaceful souls dwell in this world like the spring season, bringing welfare to all. Having themselves crossed the terrible ocean of worldly existence, they help others cross too, without any selfish motive.*
> — Vivekachudamani, Verse 39

## The Teaching

You have met someone like this. Maybe it was a grandparent who never raised their voice but whose presence made everything feel safe. Maybe it was a teacher who did not just teach a subject but somehow taught you to believe in yourself. Maybe it was a stranger on a train whose quiet composure made you suddenly aware of your own inner chaos. These people do not announce their wisdom. They do not build personal brands around it. They simply are — and everything around them shifts.

Shankaracharya compares such souls to the spring season. Spring does not arrive with a manifesto. It does not argue with winter. It simply comes, and by its nature, frozen things begin to thaw. Barren trees flower. Dormant life stirs. Spring transforms not by force but by presence. This is how the truly awakened move through the world — not converting, not conquering, but quietly changing the internal weather of everyone they encounter.

The verse adds a crucial detail: these souls have already crossed the ocean themselves. Their peace is not theoretical. They have navigated the same storms of doubt, fear, desire, and confusion that you are navigating right now. They reached the other shore and then — here is the remarkable part — they turned back. Not because they had to. Not for followers or fame. But because compassion, in the awakened, is as natural and motiveless as breathing.

This verse also contains a subtle teaching about what authentic guidance looks like. In a world saturated with self-proclaimed gurus, influencers, and thought leaders, Shankaracharya offers a diagnostic: genuine teachers do not create dependency. They do not need your admiration. They are like spring — they help you bloom into what you already are, and then they move on, asking nothing in return.

## Why This Matters

Modern life is starved of genuine presence. We interact with curated personas, optimized content, and transactional relationships. Even our closest connections often operate on an unspoken economy of emotional exchange — I give you attention so you give me validation. The exhaustion of this constant performance is one of the defining maladies of our time.

When you encounter someone who wants nothing from you — who is simply present, simply at peace — it is jarring. It interrupts your default settings. For a moment, you glimpse what life might feel like without the constant negotiation. That glimpse is what Shankaracharya is pointing toward: not just as something to seek in others, but as something to cultivate in yourself. The world does not need more content. It needs more springs.

## Today's Practice

**Becoming Spring** (Two parts)

*Part One — Gratitude (5 minutes):*
1. Think of one person who brought genuine peace into your life simply by being who they were. Not because they gave you advice or fixed your problems, but because their presence itself was nourishing.
2. Write them a message today. It does not need to be long: "I was thinking about you and wanted you to know that your presence has meant more to me than I have ever said." Send it.

*Part Two — Practice (10 minutes):*
1. For the next interaction you have — with a colleague, a family member, a barista — drop your agenda. Do not try to be impressive, funny, or efficient.
2. Simply be present. Listen fully. Let there be pauses. Do not fill silence with noise.
3. Afterwards, notice: did something shift in the interaction? Did you feel a different quality of connection?
4. Consider: what would it mean to move through your entire day this way — not performing, just being?

## Reflection

*What would change in your life if you stopped trying to be impressive and started simply being present?*
