# Who Am I? — The Original Self-Inquiry

> स्वस्वरूपानुसन्धानं भक्तिरित्यभिधीयते
>
> *"The continuous inquiry into one's own true nature — that is called devotion."*
> — Vivekachudamani, Verse 32

## What This Practice Is

This is Shankaracharya's core practice — the single method he considered more powerful than any ritual, any prayer, any yoga posture. It is radically simple: ask yourself "Who am I?" and stay with whatever arises.

This is not a philosophical puzzle to solve with the intellect. It is an experiential inquiry — you are looking for the one who is looking. You are not trying to find an answer. You are trying to dissolve the questioner.

Modern psychology calls this "decentering" — the ability to observe your thoughts without being fused with them. Shankaracharya went further: he said that when you look for the observer, you discover something that was never born, never changes, and cannot be harmed.

## Before You Begin

- Find a quiet place. Sit comfortably — chair, cushion, floor, it doesn't matter.
- Close your eyes gently. Take three deep breaths to settle.
- You don't need to stop thinking. Thoughts will come. That's fine. You're not fighting them — you're investigating who is having them.

## The Practice (15 minutes)

### Phase 1: Settling (3 minutes)

Sit quietly with eyes closed. Notice your breathing without trying to change it. Let your body relax. Let your attention come inward.

If your mind is noisy, that's normal. Just notice the noise like you'd notice traffic outside a window. You are not the traffic.

### Phase 2: The Inquiry (9 minutes)

Now, gently ask yourself:

**"Who am I?"**

Don't answer with a label. If "I am a teacher" arises — ask: "Who is the one who is a teacher?"
If "I am a thinker" arises — ask: "Who is the one thinking?"
If "I am anxious" arises — ask: "Who is the one aware of anxiety?"

Keep pointing the question inward. Every answer that comes is something you can observe — which means it is an object, not the subject. You are looking for the subject.

What you'll notice: there is something that is aware of every thought, every sensation, every identity — but itself has no qualities. It is like the sky: everything appears within it, but the sky itself remains untouched.

If you hit a gap — a moment of pure awareness without thought — stay there. Don't rush to fill it. That gap is what Shankara calls your true nature.

If you feel confused or frustrated, that's the practice working. The mind doesn't like being investigated. It prefers to investigate other things. Turning the light of attention back on itself feels disorienting at first. Stay with it.

### Phase 3: Resting (3 minutes)

Release the question. Don't try to hold onto any experience or state. Simply rest in whatever is present.

Notice: even now, there is an awareness that is effortlessly present. It was here before the practice, it's here during, and it will be here after. You didn't create it. You can't destroy it.

Open your eyes slowly. Let the room come back. Notice that the same awareness that was present with eyes closed is present now with eyes open.

## What You Might Experience

- **Nothing dramatic**: This is actually the most common and most valuable experience. The ordinary awareness you already have IS it. You just never noticed.
- **A gap in thought**: Brief moments where thinking stops and pure awareness remains. Don't chase these — they're signposts, not the destination.
- **Frustration**: "I can't find the 'I'!" — Congratulations. That's exactly the point. The separate "I" you were looking for doesn't exist.
- **Tears**: Sometimes when the mental armor softens, old emotions surface. Let them. They're not a problem — they're layers dissolving.
- **Peace**: A quiet, groundless peace that doesn't depend on circumstances. This is what Shankara calls your natural state.

## Carry It With You

Today, whenever you notice yourself reacting — to stress, to a notification, to someone's opinion of you — pause for 3 seconds and ask:

*"Who is the one reacting?"*

You don't need the answer. The question itself is the practice.

## The Key Insight

You are not looking for something new. You are recognizing what has always been here — the awareness that is reading these words right now. It has never not been present. It has never changed. It was here when you were five years old, it's here now, and it will be here when this body is gone.

Shankaracharya's radical message: you don't need to *become* anything. You need to *recognize* what you already are.
