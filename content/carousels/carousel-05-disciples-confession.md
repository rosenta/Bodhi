## Slide 1 (Cover)
**Text**: Feeling completely lost might be the most intelligent thing you have ever done.
**Visual**: Dark, foggy background. A single figure standing at a crossroads or in an open, directionless space. Not dramatic -- quiet, honest. Bold sans-serif text overlaid.

## Slide 2
**Text**: In the Vivekachudamani, a student approaches the guru and says something that will sound painfully familiar. Verse 51: "What is bondage? How did it arise? How does it persist? How can one be free? What is the not-Self? What is the Supreme Self? And how does one discriminate between them? Please tell me."
**Visual**: The verse in Devanagari script, elegant and centered. Below it, the English translation in a slightly smaller, softer font. Dark background, gold and white text. Let the words carry the weight.

## Slide 3
**Text**: Read that again. This is not a calm, composed seeker politely asking for wisdom. This is someone in crisis. Someone who has tried everything and hit a wall. Someone who is done pretending they have it figured out. Sound familiar?
**Visual**: Close-up of text messages or journal entries -- modern, relatable fragments: "I don't know who I am anymore." "What's the point of any of this?" "I feel like I'm performing my own life." Dark phone screen aesthetic. Raw, not polished.

## Slide 4
**Text**: Shankara does not judge the student. He does not say "meditate more" or "read this book." He says (verse 45): "Fear not. There is no danger for you. There is a way to cross this ocean. I shall show you the very path by which the great sages have crossed before."
**Visual**: A hand reaching down toward the viewer -- not divine or glowing, just human and steady. Dark background. The gesture is simple: I am here. I have got you. Warm, grounded tones.

## Slide 5
**Text**: Here is what most people miss: the student's crisis is not the OBSTACLE to wisdom. It IS the beginning of wisdom. Shankara treats these questions as proof that the student is ready. You do not ask "Who am I?" when you are comfortable. You ask it when everything you thought you were has stopped working.
**Visual**: A cracked surface (concrete, glass, dried earth) with light visible through the cracks. Not a "beautiful broken" aesthetic -- more structural. The crack is where the light enters. Dark, minimal.

## Slide 6
**Text**: "What is bondage?" -- this is the question underneath your anxiety. Not "why am I anxious?" but "what is it that feels trapped?" "How did it arise?" -- this is the question underneath your patterns. Not "how do I fix myself?" but "how did I become something that needs fixing?"
**Visual**: Two columns. Left: the student's original questions in Sanskrit/English. Right: their modern equivalents. Clean, parallel layout. Dark background with muted gold highlights connecting the pairs.

## Slide 7
**Text**: "What is the not-Self?" -- this is the question underneath every identity crisis. Every moment you have felt like a fraud, like the person everyone sees is not the real you -- that feeling is not dysfunction. It is data. You are sensing the gap between who you ARE and who you have been PERFORMING.
**Visual**: A mirror reflecting something different from what stands before it. Not surreal or fantastical -- subtle. The reflection is slightly shifted, slightly off. The visual unease makes the point.

## Slide 8
**Text**: "How does one discriminate between them?" -- this is the core question. Not "give me the answer." But "teach me to SEE." The student is not asking for a belief system. They are asking for a skill. Viveka -- discrimination -- the ability to tell the real from the unreal in real time, in your own experience.
**Visual**: A pair of eyes, clear and focused. Not mystical or dreamy. Alert. The visual conveys: this is about seeing, not believing. Dark background, high contrast.

## Slide 9
**Text**: So if you are reading this and you feel lost, confused, or like the life you have built does not match what you actually are -- Shankara has a message for you from 1,200 years ago: That feeling is not a bug. It is a feature. It is your intelligence refusing to accept a life lived on autopilot. Follow the discomfort. It knows where to go.
**Visual**: The foggy crossroads from Slide 1, but now with a faint path becoming visible through the fog. Not a clear road -- just enough direction to take the next step. The visual arc completes: from lost to "there is a way."

## Caption
In the Vivekachudamani, a student approaches the guru in total crisis: "What is bondage? How did it arise? How can I be free? Who am I?"

Sound familiar?

Shankara does not tell the student to calm down. He says: "Fear not. There is a way. Others have crossed this exact darkness, and the path still exists."

The student's confusion is not the obstacle. It is the prerequisite. You do not ask "Who am I?" from a place of comfort. You ask it when everything you thought you were stops working.

If that is where you are right now -- you are not lost. You are at the beginning.

Which of the student's questions hits you hardest today?
