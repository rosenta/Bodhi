# Hindi Craft Brief — Project Bodhi Stories

## 1. Why the English v2 sample was "still not naive enough"

The v2 English is genuinely good prose, but it is still **performing literariness**. Symptoms:

- **Metaphors doing emotional work the scene should do on its own.** "a mirror you mistook for a face," "scooped out and stupid in the dusk," "as if it might reconsider." These are writerly — lovely, but they ask the reader to admire the sentence before feeling the moment. A naive voice trusts the object (rope, pulse, jute) and gets out of the way.
- **Philosophical wrap-up still sounds like a line.** "You don't love the thing. You love the version of yourself that shows up when the thing is there." — this reads *correct*. It reads *authored*. A friend telling you this over chai would fumble it once, correct himself, then land it flatter: not less true, just less lacquered.
- **The Guru is still slightly oracular.** Phrases like "the Self is the only thing that was ever beloved" — beautiful, but Guru-as-quotable. The mandate says silence teaches harder. v2 still has the Guru doing 30% too much talking and 100% too much finishing thoughts.
- **No code-switch.** The disciple is a 41-year-old exec in the original — he *thinks* in English-Hindi mix. v2 sanitised him into one register. That flattens him.

So: v2 isn't naive-enough because it still wants to be admired as prose. The Hindi version deliberately de-lacquers — shorter sentences, fewer adjectives, and English words where a real person would code-switch.

## 2. Voice / register choices for Hindi

**Target reader:** urban Indian, 22–40, reads in Hindi by choice but speaks a Hindi-English mix. Has a LinkedIn. Has read one Osho book. Scrolls Instagram in Hindi memes and English captions. Does NOT want textbook Hindi.

**Register ladder I'm staying on:**

| Too high (avoid) | Target | Too low (avoid) |
|---|---|---|
| शुद्ध हिंदी / Doordarshan | Good Hindi journalism + a friend's voice-note | WhatsApp forward / cringe memes |
| "ठहरिए! सर्प है!" | "रुक. साँप है." | "अबे रुक भाई snake है" |
| "आत्मज्ञान की ओर अग्रसर" | "आत्मा ही एकमात्र है…" | (skipped entirely) |

**Specific moves I made:**

1. **Concrete first paragraph, zero interiority.** Opens on the rope, not on his mind. Physical before psychological.
2. **One idea per line. Lots of white space.** Hindi on screen reads dense; breaks do the work commas can't.
3. **Code-switch where a real person would:** `performing`, `nervous system`, `Quarterly reports`, `competitor`, `Slack`-adjacent vocabulary stays English. Translating `performing` to `अभिनय` would be a lie about how this man thinks.
4. **Sanskrit used sparingly, never explained in-line.** `आत्मा` appears once. `माया` doesn't appear in the first 400 words at all — it's earned later in the scene, not front-loaded. Banned list (`आत्मज्ञान`, `परमात्मा`, `ज्ञानी`, `साधक`, `मोक्ष`) — zero usages.
5. **Dialogue is natural, clipped, often ungrammatical-on-purpose.** "रुक. साँप है." / "किस से?" / "साँप से." / "चल." — the Guru uses तू, because real teachers who love you do.
6. **Guru speaks less.** Longest Guru turn in the 400w is ~2 sentences. Silence (किंगफ़िशर, पानी) carries weight.
7. **Specificity:** पीपल का पेड़, जूट की रस्सी, किंगफ़िशर — not generic पेड़/रस्सी/पक्षी.
8. **No shuddh-Hindi flex.** नब्ज़ not नाड़ी. नदी के किनारे not नदी-तट. धुँधली not मंद.

## 3. Keep-worthy line (standalone / screenshot)

> **"महसूस करना बंद नहीं करना है. महसूस को सबूत मानना बंद करना है."**

This is the landing. It works on its own, doesn't require knowing the rope-snake story, and it's sayable — the kind of line someone forwards at 1 AM.

## 4. Before / After — one sentence

**English v2:**
> "You've been afraid. The fear was real. Your hands shook. The danger was not. That is the whole teaching. You don't have to stop feeling. You have to stop trusting the feeling as evidence."

**Hindi v1 rendering:**
> "तू डरा था. डर असली था. हाथ काँपे थे. ख़तरा नहीं था. पूरी बात यही है. महसूस करना बंद नहीं करना है. महसूस को सबूत मानना बंद करना है."

**What changed in the translation:**
- `the feeling as evidence` → `महसूस को सबूत मानना` — kept `सबूत` (standard Urdu-Hindi) over `प्रमाण` (Sanskritised). `सबूत` is the word a lawyer or a cop or your friend would actually use.
- Used `तू` — the Guru's intimacy, not distance. `तुम` would be neutral-respectful; `आप` would be wrong for this relationship.
- Kept the same telegraphic rhythm. Each sentence a breath.

## 5. Craft rules for future Hindi stories (refined)

1. **Open on an object, not an emotion.** First sentence = something a camera could shoot.
2. **One idea per sentence.** If a sentence has two clauses joined by और/लेकिन, ask if they should be two sentences. Usually yes.
3. **Code-switch is a feature, not a failure.** Keep English words (tech, corporate, modern life) when the character would. Never force-translate `notifications` → `सूचनाएँ`.
4. **Sanskrit is gravity, not decoration.** One Sanskrit term per 500 words is plenty. `आत्मा`, `माया`, `विवेक`, `कर्म` are shared vocabulary — use as-is. Banned (unless scene demands): `आत्मज्ञान`, `परमात्मा`, `ज्ञानी`, `साधक`, `मोक्ष`. If you use `मोक्ष`, only once, at a load-bearing moment.
5. **Dialogue = how people actually talk.** Contractions, fragments, interjections. The Guru uses `तू` with the disciple (intimacy). The disciple uses `आप` or nothing (respect, often just the unsaid subject).
6. **Specificity over abstraction.** `पीपल का पेड़`, not `पेड़`. `जूट की रस्सी`, not `रस्सी`. `किंगफ़िशर`, not `पक्षी`. `Quarterly report`, not `त्रैमासिक रिपोर्ट`.
7. **Guru speaks ≤ 3 sentences per turn.** Silence is a character. Use ambient sound (river, bird, dust) where the English would want another Guru line.
8. **Keep-worthy line check.** Every story (per ~400w) should have at least one sentence that could stand alone as a screenshot. If you can't find one, the story is too smooth — nothing is sticking.
9. **Urdu-Hindi words welcome.** `ख़तरा`, `नब्ज़`, `सबूत`, `वक़्त`, `हक़ीक़त` — these are the Hindi people actually use. Sanskritised alternatives (`संकट`, `नाड़ी`, `प्रमाण`, `समय`, `यथार्थ`) sound like a textbook.
10. **No italics / no transliteration for Sanskrit and English words in-line.** If the character would say `performing` in Roman English, write `performing`. If they would say `आत्मा`, write `आत्मा` in Devanagari. Trust the reader — they switch scripts in their own head all day.

## 6. Open questions for user (nice to clarify before full rewrite)

- **तू vs तुम for the Guru.** I chose `तू` for intimacy. If Project Bodhi wants the Guru at slightly more distance (warm but formal), switch to `तुम`. `आप` is too cold for this dynamic.
- **How much English in dialogue?** I kept `performing`, `Quarterly reports`, `competitor`, `nervous system`. I'd be comfortable adding `board meeting`, `KPI`, `notifications`, `Slack` in later scenes. User should flag if this is too much.
- **Single-quote vs double-quote style for dialogue in Hindi markdown** — I used double quotes `"…"` matching the English. Some Hindi publishers prefer `'…'` or guillemets. Flag preference.
