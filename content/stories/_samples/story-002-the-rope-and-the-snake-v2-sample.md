# The Rope and the Snake

The rope was coiled beside the path.

He saw it at the edge of the fading light — thick, dark, patterned in the way vipers are patterned when they are about to move. His heart slammed twice against his sternum. He grabbed the old man's sleeve.

"Stop. Snake."

The Guru looked. Walked forward. Picked it up.

Jute. Frayed at one end. Someone had tied a boat to the peepal above them and the knot had slipped.

The disciple's hands were shaking. He could feel the adrenaline draining out of him, leaving him scooped out and stupid in the dusk.

"Feel your chest," the Guru said.

"It's still pounding."

"From what?"

"The snake."

"There was no snake." The old man let the rope drop. It made a soft sound in the dust. "Only rope. And low light. And a nervous system doing what it was built to do." He turned and kept walking along the riverbank. "Come."

They walked. The disciple's pulse took a long time to come down. He kept glancing back at the rope as if it might reconsider.

"An hour ago," the Guru said, "I asked you what you loved most."

"My daughter."

"Why?"

"Because when I'm with her, I stop performing."

"So you love her. Or you love who you get to be around her?"

He opened his mouth. Closed it. A kingfisher called once across the water and didn't call again.

"Every love is like that," the Guru said. "You don't love the thing. You love the version of yourself that shows up when the thing is there." He glanced sideways. "The Self is the only thing that was ever beloved. Everything else is a mirror you mistook for a face."

The disciple thought about his company. The quarterly reports. The way his jaw tightened when a competitor launched anything. Forty-one years of snakes. Every one of them rope.

"What did you just figure out?" the Guru asked.

"That I've been afraid of things that weren't there."

"No." The old man stopped walking. "You've been afraid. The fear was real. Your hands shook. The danger was not. That is the whole teaching. You don't have to stop feeling. You have to stop trusting the feeling as evidence."
