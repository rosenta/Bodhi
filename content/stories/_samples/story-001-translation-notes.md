# Story 001 — Translation Notes

## What I CUT from the English

1. **The opening resume paragraph** ("He had graduated at the top of his class...") was compressed from 80 words to a 12-line rhythmic list. The English builds the man's worldly success through accumulation; Hindi oral storytelling trusts the listener to infer from fewer, sharper items.
2. **The full "he tried everything" paragraph** (therapy, retreats, philosophers) — English runs 90 words, Hindi is 45. I kept the concrete objects (silent retreat, Meditation app) and cut the connective tissue.
3. **The Guru's final warning closing block** — in English it runs: corner office → tailored shirts → "I know" → stepped inside. I kept all four beats but stripped adjectives and commentary. The ending lands once, then stops.
4. **The "wellness influencer" line** stayed — it was too good to lose, and it code-switches naturally in Hindi urban speech.
5. **The long "What is bondage? How did it arise?..." paragraph** kept its italicized treatment but I did not add a Hindi explanation. The reader earns it through context.

## What I ADDED (sensory specifics English lacked)

1. **The Milan pantsuit detail** — "Milan की पतलून" — grounds the man's class and the mud's insult in one beat. English was abstract about his wealth.
2. **Slack notification + iPhone** — anchors him in 2024 urban tech-exec reality. English said "his calendar was booked three months in advance"; Hindi shows the phone buzzing unanswered.
3. **IIT** — specific to the Indian reader. "Top of the class" is translated but given a local marker.
4. **"आम के पेड़"** — mango trees, kept from the farmer's direction. Hindi readers feel this more than "the mangoes grow".
5. **Morning ash still warm** ("राख अभी गरम थी") — the English said "embers still glowed". Warmth is what a Hindi listener registers.

## Keep-worthy line (both languages)

**Hindi:** जो तू ढूँढ रहा है, उसकी क़ीमत वही है जो तू अभी अपने आप को समझता है.

**English source:** "What you are looking for will cost you everything you think you are."

**Why it lands harder in Hindi:** The English hides the threat in "cost you everything." Hindi makes it brutal and intimate through `तू` — the Guru is not speaking to a seeker, he is speaking to *this man*. The word `क़ीमत` (price, Urdu-Hindi) is the word a shopkeeper or a contract-killer uses. It is transactional, unsentimental. Combined with `जो तू अभी अपने आप को समझता है` (what you currently think yourself to be), it turns self-identity into a commodity on the counter.

## Before / After — two dramatic pairs

### Pair 1: The opening

**English:**
> "The man who knelt at the edge of the river had won everything. He had graduated at the top of his class. He had built a company that six hundred people depended on. He had a wife who was kind, children who were healthy, a house that strangers photographed from the street."

**Hindi:**
> "घुटने गीले थे.
>
> पतलून की crease पर गंगा की मिट्टी लग गई थी — महँगी पतलून, Milan की — और उसे फ़र्क़ नहीं पड़ रहा था. पहली बार पिछले बीस साल में, उसे फ़र्क़ नहीं पड़ रहा था."

The English opens with a verdict ("had won everything"). Hindi opens with wet knees. The resume comes later, in its own section, as a flashback beat. The physical precedes the psychological — craft rule #1.

### Pair 2: The Guru's eyes

**English:**
> "These eyes were different. They were clear and sharp and absolutely unafraid, and they looked at him the way a surgeon looks at an X-ray — not with judgment, but with a precision that saw through skin and muscle to the bone beneath."

**Hindi:**
> "ये आँखें अलग थीं. साफ़. तेज़. बिल्कुल बेख़ौफ़. और उन्होंने उसे उस तरह देखा जैसे surgeon X-ray देखता है — judge नहीं करता, बस देख लेता है. चमड़ी के पार. हड्डी तक."

The English runs one sentence of 40 words. Hindi breaks it into seven beats. Each one a breath. `surgeon` and `judge` stay English — this is how a 41-year-old urban exec thinks. "चमड़ी के पार. हड्डी तक." is a two-beat landing that the English smooths over with a comma.

## Risks / flags for review

- I used `शिष्य` throughout in the frontmatter, following the pattern of story-002. The word `साधक` is on the banned list. `शिष्य` is neutral and correct.
- I used `तू` for the Guru → disciple, matching story-002's intimacy choice. If user wants more distance, switch to `तुम`.
- The dadi-ka-book moment is slightly more foregrounded in Hindi than English. I judged this earns its place because it localizes the verse-discovery in a way Indian readers will feel.
- One Sanskrit loan in the quoted verse: `आत्मा और अनात्मा का विवेक`. Within the 500-word-per-term budget. Not explained inline.
