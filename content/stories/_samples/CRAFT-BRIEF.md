## One-line diagnoses (6)

**Story 001 — The Arrival**
Opens on concept (`had won everything`) plus a resume recap; reader has nothing to picture for 80 words and by the time we reach the river we've been told, not shown, a midlife crisis we've seen in 200 essays.

**Story 002 — The Rope and the Snake**
Buries the actual parable (the rope moment) behind 400 words of walk-and-talk abstraction about "loving the Self"; the shareable image lands too late and the Guru does the reader's interpretive work for them.

**Story 003 — Peeling the Onion**
Guru delivers five consecutive mini-lectures with Sanskrit footnotes (`annamaya kosha`, `pranamaya kosha`) glossed in-dialogue; the disciple's only job is to be a setup line — silence would teach harder than the itemized speech.

**Story 004 — The Question That Broke Everything**
The hourly rhythm (`After the first hour... After the second hour... After the third hour`) is a crutch that skips the specificity of what three hours of self-inquiry actually feels like; also over-lands the ending with a TED-Talk exit line ("You found the one who was asking").

**Story 005 — Spring Returns**
Final 300 words are the author explaining the metaphor that the preceding 1,200 words already made; kills the keep-worthy moment by circling it three times ("It came like spring" → "spring had come" → "like all true springs").

**Story 006 — The Five Rooms**
Premise is schematic — literally five rooms for five sheaths — so the prose has to carry the whole thing on sensory specificity, but it defaults to abstract beats ("warmth that seemed to radiate from everywhere and nowhere"); the structure needs the strongest writing and currently gets the vaguest.

## Which story I sampled and why

**Sampled: Story 002 — The Rope and the Snake.**

Two reasons:
1. **Highest platform potential.** The rope-snake is the Vedanta parable most likely to screenshot, reel, and quote-card. If we get one story right for social, this is it. The V1 buries the image under theory.
2. **Worst diagnosis mismatch.** The craft failure in V1 (abstraction before image) is the same failure that makes it un-shareable. Fixing the craft fixes the platform problem in one pass.

Rewrite focuses on: opening on the rope (concrete image, not concept), cutting the "objects are not loved for their own sake" speech into two lines of actual dialogue, pulling the parable forward to the first paragraph, and ending on a line the reader can screenshot.

Sample delivers the V1's ~600-word arc in ~400 words. File: `content/stories/_samples/story-002-the-rope-and-the-snake-v2-sample.md`

## The money comparison — one paragraph, V1 → V2

### V1 opening (first ~110 words)

> On the seventh evening, the Guru asked the disciple to walk with him. They moved along the river path as the sun bled out along the horizon, painting the water the color of copper. The disciple had spent the week in silence — sitting, watching, trying not to think, and failing. His mind was a fist that would not unclench. Every time he closed his eyes, the spreadsheets came back, the emails, the phantom buzzing of a phone he had left ten thousand miles away.
>
> "Tell me," the Guru said, stepping over a root with the ease of a man who had walked this path ten thousand times, "what do you love most in your life?"

**What's failing:** Opens on schedule ("seventh evening"), then a sunset cliché ("sun bled out"), then a metaphor-stack for the disciple's mind ("fist that would not unclench") before anything *happens*. Reader is 110 words in and the only concrete thing is a phone they don't have. The parable — the actual reason to read this story — is still 400 words away.

### V2 opening (first ~110 words)

> The rope was coiled beside the path.
>
> He saw it at the edge of the fading light — thick, dark, patterned in the way vipers are patterned when they are about to move. His heart slammed twice against his sternum. He grabbed the old man's sleeve.
>
> "Stop. Snake."
>
> The Guru looked. Walked forward. Picked it up.
>
> Jute. Frayed at one end. Someone had tied a boat to the peepal above them and the knot had slipped.

**What's working:** First line is the object the whole story is about. Sensory before interior. "Heart slammed twice against his sternum" gives a body, not a metaphor. "Jute. Frayed at one end." — three-word sentences as a craft choice: the disciple's brain is catching up. "Peepal" (not "tree") names the specific ficus religiosa a reader in Delhi or Dallas can both see. The parable lands in paragraph three, not on page two.

**Keep-worthy line from the sample** (candidate for quote card):
> "You don't have to stop feeling. You have to stop trusting the feeling as evidence."

## Craft rules applied (audit)

- Opens on concrete image (rope), not concept — yes
- One idea per sentence, generous white space — yes
- Zero banned words (sacred / divine / journey / awakening / essence / volcanic / terrible / sacred container) — yes
- Adjectives cut unless earning keep (`thick, dark, patterned` — earning; `fading light` — earning) — yes
- Dialogue uses contractions, half-thoughts ("Stop. Snake." / "From what?" / "The snake.") — yes
- Specificity: peepal, jute, kingfisher, sternum — yes
- No TED-Talk exit; ends on a line that reframes without explaining — yes
- Covers the V1 arc in ~400 words vs ~600 — yes
