# The Wife at the Threshold

He had packed the bag at three in the morning because he did not trust himself to pack it in daylight.

One change of clothes. A toothbrush. The slim book with the cracked spine his grandmother had given him, still unread. His passport. A stack of bills he had already signed. That was all. He had stood in the closet for a long time looking at the shirts -- the tailored ones, the cashmere sweaters, the wool coat that had cost more than his first car -- and he had understood, with a clarity that had nothing to do with him, that none of it would be coming. Clothes were for a man who knew who he was. He did not know anymore.

He carried the bag downstairs and set it by the door. Then he sat at the kitchen table and waited for her to wake up.

She came down at six, the way she always did, in the blue robe that was beginning to fray at the cuffs. She stopped when she saw him sitting in the dark. She saw the bag. She saw his face. And she did not say anything for a long time.

Then she made tea.

She moved around the kitchen the way she had moved around it for nineteen years -- the kettle, the cups, the small ceramic bowl of cardamom pods. He watched her hands. He had watched her hands at the altar when they married, and at the hospital when their daughter was born, and in the hundred thousand small mornings since. He had never really looked at them, he realized. He had looked past them the way you look past a window to the view beyond.

She set the tea in front of him. She sat down across the table.

"How long," she said. Not a question. A request for a number.

"I don't know."

"Six months. A year."

"I don't know."

She looked at him. Her eyes were dry. He had braced himself for tears and the absence of them was somehow worse.

"Will you come back?"

He wanted to lie to her. The lie was ready on his tongue -- *of course, of course, I'll call you every week, this is temporary* -- and he swallowed it because she deserved better than that, and because he did not actually know.

"I don't know that either," he said.

She nodded slowly. She picked up her tea and held it in both hands, the way she did when she was cold, though the kitchen was warm.

"I knew this was coming," she said.

"How?"

"Four months ago. You were at the dinner for the Patels' anniversary, and someone asked you what you did, and you opened your mouth to say CEO and nothing came out. I saw it. You covered. You said the title. But I saw the pause." She looked at him with an expression he could not read. "I have seen you lose many things in nineteen years. Deals. Friends. Your father. I had never seen you lose your own title in your mouth before. That was when I knew."

"Why didn't you say anything?"

"Because it wasn't mine to say." She set down the cup. "And because I loved you too much to try to stop it."

Something in him broke that he had not known was still whole.

"I'm not leaving you," he said, and his voice was rough. "I need you to hear that. I am not leaving you. I am --"

"You are leaving yourself," she said. "The version of you that you have been wearing. I know."

He could not speak. He reached across the table and took her hand, and she let him, and they sat like that -- two people who had built a life together, holding hands over the wreckage of the story they had agreed to believe.

"You know what I used to wonder?" she said after a long time. "When we first married. I used to wonder what would happen if you ever actually got quiet. You were always so loud in your own head, always running, always building. I used to think -- if he ever sits still for one whole day, what will he find?" She looked at him with that unreadable expression. "I think we are about to find out."

He laughed -- a short, broken sound that was half a sob.

"I am so afraid," he said. "I don't know what I am doing. I don't know what I am looking for. I just know I cannot keep pretending."

"Good," she said. "Pretending was what was killing you. I watched it. For years. It was like watching someone slowly drown in a swimming pool on land."

He wiped his face with the back of his hand.

"The house is paid," he said. "The accounts -- everything is in your name too. You don't need to --"

"I don't care about the house."

"The company will --"

"I don't care about the company."

She stood up. She walked around the table and stood behind him and put her hands on his shoulders, and he felt her forehead come to rest against the back of his head, and he closed his eyes.

"Listen to me," she said quietly. "Whatever you are going to find -- I want you to find it all the way. Do you understand me? Do not come back halfway. Do not come back a man who went to the mountain and brought back a motto. If you are going to do this, do it fully. Break yourself open. Find the thing. And if the thing you find is that you cannot come back here -- I will survive that. I want you to know I will survive that. I will grieve and I will survive. But I will not survive watching you shrink back into the life you are already too big for."

He could not answer. He reached up and held her hands where they rested on his shoulders.

"She is going to ask where you are," his wife said.

"I know."

"I am not going to lie to her."

"I don't want you to."

"I am going to tell her that her father went to look for something he could not find here. And that he loved her enough to go look."

"Tell her," he said, and his voice broke, "tell her I am not running away. Tell her I am running toward. Tell her -- tell her when I come back, if I come back, it will be with more of me, not less."

She bent her head against his and held him like that, and he could feel her breath on his neck.

"Sons and others can repay a father's debts," she said softly -- she had found the book, sometime in the last four months; she had been reading it; he had not known -- "but there is no one other than oneself who can remove one's own bondage. I read that verse. I read it over and over. I wanted it not to be true. I wanted there to be a way I could do this for you. But there isn't. Is there."

"No."

"Then go."

He stood up. She stepped back. He looked at her in the blue robe with the fraying cuffs, and he memorized her. The small line between her brows. The gray at her temple. The exact way she was standing, braced against something, a woman who had known this morning was coming and had dressed for it.

He climbed the stairs and went to his daughter's room and stood in the doorway. She was sleeping with one arm flung over her head, the way she had slept since she was two. He did not go in. He did not trust himself. He watched her breathe, in and out, in and out, and he thought: *among all living beings, human birth is the rarest. And she is rarer still. And I am leaving her to go find out why.*

He came back down. His wife was standing at the door holding his bag.

She handed it to him. He took it.

"Go," she said. And then, "Come back changed, or don't come back."

He walked out onto the porch. The sky was beginning to lighten in the east, the first gray seeping in behind the black trees. He heard the door close behind him. He heard the lock turn -- not to keep him out, he understood, but to let her be alone with what had just happened to her.

He stood on the porch in the cold, and he thought he understood, for the first time in his life, what the old books meant when they said the journey begins with renunciation. It was not the renunciation of objects. The house was still his. The money was still his. The clothes hung in the closet still waiting. None of that had changed.

What he had renounced was the right to pretend it had been enough.

He walked down the steps. The car was in the drive. The taxi he had ordered was supposed to come at six-thirty. He had eleven minutes. He sat down on the porch step and he put his face in his hands and he let himself cry, quietly, for the life he was leaving and for the woman inside the house who had loved him fiercely enough to open the door and show him the way out.

The taxi came. He got in. He did not look back at the house because he did not need to. He carried the shape of her in the blue robe with him now, the way a man carries water across a desert -- carefully, gratefully, knowing it is finite and knowing it is his.

The cab driver asked where he was going.

He gave the airport.

The driver asked where he was flying.

He gave the country. The city. The road leading upstream, beside the river, to where the mangoes grow.

The driver whistled softly. "Long way."

"Yes," he said. He looked out the window at the empty streets of the life he was leaving. "Yes it is."

The first light touched the tops of the buildings. The car turned the corner. The house disappeared behind him, and in the kitchen, his wife sat alone at the table with two cups of cold tea and one unread book, and she did not move for a long time, and she did not weep, and she did not regret, because love that does not know when to let go is not love but a kind of holding that becomes a cage.

She had opened the cage. She had watched the bird go.

Somewhere, she believed, he would learn to fly. And whether he came back to her wrist or not, the sky would have him, and that would be enough.

It would have to be.

She picked up the book her husband had left behind -- the slim volume with the cracked spine, the grandmother's gift, the thing he had forgotten on the kitchen counter in his fastidious, terrible haste -- and she held it in both hands and she read the first verse again, the one about the rarity of human birth, and she thought:

*And rarer still is the one who lets the seeker go.*
